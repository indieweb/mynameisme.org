My Name is The Dod

My name is Nimrod Shabtai Keret. For years, I’ve been an activist in Israel (drug laws, occupation, privacy). I still do many things, but that’s personal

In most online communities, my pseudonym is The Dod (or @TheRealDod), but I have other nicknames. Some are publicly associated with my real life identity (my Twitter profile leads to my site), some don’t; It’s my right to choose whether they do or not.

My demands for anonymity extend beyond having a single pseudonym. I think it’s a basic human right to have more than one. Every gay teacher who goes through fertility treatments and every politically active engineer who uses a dating service has a right to have at least 3 different identities.

The fact that we’re being brainwashed to believe that having more than one identity is a sin called “trolling”, is dangerous to society. Of course, anonymity can be abused, just like a hammer or any other tool. Privacy is an essential tool: a world without it is far worse than – say – a world without hammers.

Identity is not a must unless you’re a dictator’s minion. Accountability should be enough: all *you* need to know about *me* is that I’m the same “me” that was here yesterday and not an impostor. What *else* I do on the internet or in real life is none of your business.

And now for a technological rant: One of the worst things that happened to the Internet community is when proprietary single-sign-on standards (by Twitter, Google, etc.) managed to bully an anonymous accountability standard called OpenID out of the game, by not supporting it, supporting it one way, and other embrace-and-choke techniques.

Why is OpenID (or any other provider-agnostic identification standard) important?
Because a person’s accounts in dating services, politics boards, customer clubs, etc. should not only be *different*, these accounts should be managed by different *providers*. No malicious insider, hacker, or “civil servant” (with or without a warrant) should know that the teacher is gay, or that the activist uses a dating service. Even without knowing their real name – this information can still be abused online.

Not that this alone would help. We’re constantly being tracked by constantly evolving technologies with constantly devolving morality, but the last thing we need is to pour oil on this slippery slope. We can expect powerful bodies to know embarrassingly much about us, but it’s no reason to expose this information to less-equipped stalkers and criminals.

There are many threats to our privacy, and you can use many practices and technologies to avoid or minimize their damage.

Whatever you do, remember: anonymity is your right.
