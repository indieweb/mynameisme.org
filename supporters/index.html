  9. My name is... various

    I present different identities in different contexts. Some are my real name, or close to it. Some aren’t.

    I choose when I want to share or participate as me, and when I don’t.

    I’ve been a participant in online communities for nearly a quarter century, and from the beginning I’ve found people who’ve become very close friends, and those I’d prefer not to meet ever.

    As life progresses, it becomes more complex. I have circles I interact with by virtue of geographic communities, family, school, friends, work, interests and hobbies ranging from commonplace to obscure and sometimes sensitive. Some of these circles intersect, but for the most part, they don’t.

    I’ve found myself held to answer questions at work, in business, to government agencies, law enforcement, and courts. I’m very much aware of how online and electronic data can be tied together, and how a person determined to communicate a given narrative based on a set of facts can do so largely independent of the true nature and context of those facts. David Brin’s “Transparent Society” vision is hugely naive.

    I have seen negative consequences of prior online discussions I’ve had, if even only from other’s behavior in them, though I considered these conversations inconsequential at the time. (I do stand by my comments and behavior regardless.)

    As a male I’ve seen the side of relationship, sexual, and gender abuse in work, relationships, and the law. It’s something I care a great deal about but am (along with many men) reluctant to mention given the social stigmas and misunderstandings associated with this. Gender inequity and injustice don’t apply only to women, children, minorities, or those with alternate sexual orientations.

    I see, think, and comment on aspects of the world, technology, business, and law which are difficult to discuss freely when any such conversations may be instantly linked to me. There’s a long history of anonymous and pseudonymous speech in the US and elsewhere, and I believe strongly in continuing that tradition.

    I have and use many names, for many reasons. That is my choice and right.

It’s just not the name I was born with, and that’s fine with me.

    Also, a long time ago, when I was working in a call center, my employer offered us the safety of using a pseudonym/nickname on the phones. I chose one because I was still transitioning into being EstiRose, and it wasn’t hard to find me on the net then. It didn’t prevent me from being able to provide services to my callers, it just meant that they couldn’t find me easily on the internet. I didn’t want someone deciding they disliked what I said, and tracking me down at home.

    I don’t think anyone, in this era of being able to be tracked down fairly easily, wants someone to dislike what they said and decide to argue with them in person, or worse. The use of real names can be a risk, even if you’re not a political dissident. There is safety in a name that’s not your own, I think, and I’m glad I’m not the only one.
