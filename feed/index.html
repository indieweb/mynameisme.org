My name is Jamie Zawinski, though far more people know me as “jwz”. I was one of the founders of Netscape and Mozilla.org, have been involved in the open source software community since the mid-80s, and have been blogging since before that was even a thing. I ended up with the handle “jwz” because that was the custom in hacker culture at the time. It stuck.

When I introduce myself to someone as “Jamie”, the response is often, “Wait, do you mean jwz?” They often seem perplexed that I’m using that other name. Nobody knows who “Jamie” is, but a lot of people know this “jwz” person.

Over the years I’ve collaborated on and managed software projects with many hundreds of volunteer contributors, and I almost never knew any of their “real” names. Their work and their reputations spoke for them, not the trivia of what their parents happened to call them.

When you are young, most people don’t take you seriously.

I was 15 years old when I got my first job programming. I can tell you firsthand that the shield of a pseudonym can be a useful tool to have people judge you by your skills and accomplishments rather than by whatever prejudices they bring.

There have been occasions when I learned that a long-time contributor to one of my projects was still a teenager. Doubtless there have been occasions where that was true but I never found out, because the person didn’t volunteer it, and it didn’t matter.

And that’s how it should be. “On the Internet, nobody knows you’re a dog.”

Outside of software collaborations, I’ve been a participant in many other online communities where pseudonyms were the norm rather than the exception, starting with USENET and BBSes, and including sites like LiveJournal, my own blog, and the blogs of others. The idea that somehow “real names” are more inherently trustworthy or polite flies in the face of my experience.

I am the vice-chair of Electronic Frontiers Australia, the peak online civil liberties group in Australia. I have taught digital media and web publishing at the University of Melbourne. Prior to that, I was a longtime technology journalist, blogger, and editor.

I also have a persistent pseudonym I use on several online communities which is older than the minimum age for joining Facebook. I learned how to behave in a civil manner online from communities where pseudonyms were the norm. So I know that you don’t need to use real names to have civil community online.
As an educator, I’ve long been dedicated to bridging the digital divide and making technology and the internet more accessible and our online culture more diverse. I am very concerned that mandating real names online will have a detrimental effect on free speech, resulting in less diversity in online culture and conversation, and less participation by minorities. I am very concerned that the voices of vulnerable people, such as political activists or commentators in countries without free speech protections, will be silenced if real names are required for them to participate online.

Pseudonyms can allow people to go about their business online in safety – whether that’s playing an online game, blogging their political opinions, or having a presence online without worrying that a violent ex-partner might track them down. I learned this from an extremely safe position of privilege as a middle class woman in a democratic country. My profession and hobbies mean I’ve spent a long time in male-dominated environments online (from MMO gaming to participating in tech conferences). So I know from experience that sometimes just being a visible minority can make you a target for harrassment. But there are people in the world who would risk more than I ever could, simply by using their real names, identifying with their real gender, or expressing their political beliefs. In countries with government-controlled broadcast media, the internet may be the only medium of free speech available to people.
We need to ensure that people who hold unpopular political or religious statements can continue to express those views online.

We need to remember when we create policies and laws for online behaviour, that must aim to create the best possible outcome for all users of the internet, not just our privileged selves.

Some people have argued that mandating real names is a way to make people more accountable and more civil online. While I’d love to see more civil conduct online, I do not believe that mandating the use of real names will achieve this goal. Furthermore, as we’ve seen in the beta of Google Plus, Google’s new online social network, real name policies are technically very difficult to implement successfully. There have already been numerous documented cases of people whose legal names were rejected by Google Plus, either because the person only has one legal name, or because Google’s naming system couldn’t cope with people’s non-English language names.

Attempts by governments or companies to enforce real name policies to ensure great civility or accountability online (or greater income for advertising companies) will have a disproportionate, chilling effect on free speech of the most vulnerable minorities online. The cost to free speech would far outweigh any gain in civility or online accountability.

Anonymous and pseudonymous speech as a tool for political dissent pre-dates the internet, and must be maintained in the internet age as well. As the internet plays an increasingly large role in the way we express ourselves and create our cultures and societies, we should be very concerned about companies or governments which seek to mandate real name policies online, to the detriment of free speech, personal safety and privacy.

For these reasons, I support the right to choose one’s own name online.

My name is Gwyneth Llewelyn, and I’m a virtual girl in a virtual world. I’m too old to still be called a “geek”. But back in the early 1990s I was one of the top Internet evangelists in my own country; these days, I have the same enthusiasm in promoting online virtual worlds and a lifestyle based on providing services over them, which naturally also raises the issue of identity.

In early 2004 I was stalked over the Internet. A small group of petty criminals, who were actually not very computer-literate, nevertheless managed to figure out, based on IP address and other similar, simple, easy-to-find hints, if I was at home, at work, or someplace else. This allowed them to track me down in order to blackmail me; I had earned the reputation of being rich (a rumour spread by a former associate) and this encouraged all sorts of small criminals, scammers, and others to help me to relieve me from the alleged vast fortune I had amassed during the golden days of the Internet revolution…

Because police work in my country is too slow — and the criminals know that — due to judiciary bureaucracy, I had no other option but to move far, far away from my home, leaving my contacts to my lawyer and close family, and live in a tiny rented room on a flat with little else but a bed, a tiny fridge, a micro-wave oven, and a shower in the bathroom. I had no fixed job, a salary below the poverty line, and not a single cent in my name; all I had was some clothes and a computer.

Since most of my work was done remotely, I made a decision: I would shut down all messengers, stop participating on forums, chatrooms, and similar online venues under my own name. I even stopped blogging, something I had done irregularly since 1995. Instead, I went completely pseudonymous, thanks mostly to Second Life, which I had “discovered” in mid-2004. In Second Life, nobody really knows who you are — and nobody truly cares. So slowly I started picking up all my social networking functions, including blogging, and even started participating online far more than before. But this time it was under my pseudonym.

Ironically, I even created a new company, where nobody but the partners knew my true identity. Contracts were signed with my pseudonym; meetings with customers were mostly done online (in Second Life) or just by exchanging emails or text messages. The company is still around and doing business, and very few know my real name or identity, but that was never an obstacle in providing good services and establishing a solid reputation.

I gave some live interviews (and lots of written ones) and did remote presentations using my avatar; I wrote an article for the Guardian under my pseudonym (as well for many sites as a guest blogger); I even have a few academic articles waiting to be published (and a few references from fellow researchers) and to this very day, almost all online registrations I constantly do use my pseudonym pretty much everywhere. Beyond Second Life, I’m also active in the WordPress community. The result of all this activity is the oddity that Google or Bing list ten or twenty times more results for my pseudonym than for my so-called “real” identity, which is pretty much “invisible” on the Internet.

Living for seven years under an assumed pseudonym which has a much vaster impact on the ‘net audience than my real identity started to make me question issues about identity and, ultimately, the self. Perhaps it’s no coincidence that I’ve also started to become a serious Dharma practitioner, which definitely helps one to experimentally find answers about one’s self, and these are radically opposite to what the likes of Facebook and Google think about identity

Talel was talking about the weather, some geek stuff that he likes, his job. I’m happy that today I’m recovering from this e-schizophrenia, and that I can consider my two persons as the same whether in life or on the web.

But what makes me happier is that I could continue to live my two lives, but I made the choice not to. Now I understand that the problem is not about pseudonyms vs. real names, it is just about the freedom of choice. I prefer to use my real name, others don’t, not because they don’t want to, but sometimes because they simply cannot.

Sometimes we cannot use our real names, and I will always remember the books of the Algerian author “Yassmina Khadra”, a woman with great style whose books are a real pleasure. A couple of years after her first book we discovered that this woman was in fact a man with a man’s name. Did I feel betrayed? Absolutely not. The use of a pseudonym was intended just to protect the writer, and the reading pleasure was the same; the author could through his book create a genuine link with the reader, and all I needed was a way to identify him just to buy his books; I would still be happy if he were identified with a number.

I think that such an example is proof that it doesn’t matter how you call yourself or how people call you: You have the right to be who you want to be.

And, you didn’t choose your very first name, so why should someone now prevent you from naming yourself “Superman” if you want to?

My name is Flavia Dzodan.  I live in Amsterdam, The Netherlands. I am a writer, an activist and ideas instigator who bases her political affiliations, activism, and work on the rights of minorities and immigrants living in Europe. My work has appeared in publications like The Guardian and I am also an editor at Tiger Beatdown. I have been interviewed by major Dutch publications like De Volkskrant over my views and opinions of Dutch society. Additionally, I am a member of one of Amsterdam’s main political parties, where I am active within my community to raise awareness of discrimination and institutional practices that seek to alienate those of non-Dutch origin.

I write under my government name because I wish to give visibility to the realities of being a minority and an immigrant in an environment that is growing increasingly hostile to diversity and giving preeminence to outdated notions of nationalism and xenophobia. I have the privilege of writing under my government name because I am a documented, legal resident of the country I live in. However, this privilege is not awarded to everyone.

Political refugees fear for the fate of family, friends, and communities still living in their home countries. Bearing witness to the acts that forced them to leave their homes comes with a price. In cases like these, pseudonymity is not just a matter of hiding one’s identity but of protecting the very livelihood of those left behind.

Many come to Europe seeking asylum. North African migrants arrive at the coast of European countries and are placed in temporary internment camps, only to be released at a later date without a clear legal status. While their applications are processed, the asylum seeker is left voiceless, unable to speak of their experience. This whole process can take a decade or longer. Speaking about this injustice under one’s government name comes with great risk, the asylum seeker faces further internment in a camp or deportation. In these cases, a pseudonym, a new identity, is an absolute necessity.

Immigrants, both documented and undocumented, fear the consequences of speaking up against injustice and discrimination. Many are victims of crimes that they cannot report because doing so would bring their residency claims into question. Women migrants are systematically targeted for sexual harassment and abuse because perpetrators know that the chances of police involvement are minimal. If an immigrant wishes to seek support in online communities and safe spaces, doing so under their government name would put them under the spotlight of authorities.

By enforcing a policy of “Only government names are allowed”, we are denying all of these people a voice. We are confining them to a silenced category where we remove their right to even discuss the injustices inflicted upon them. We are complicit in their double victimization.
