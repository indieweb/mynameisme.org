My name is Vorpal Bunny

My name is Vorpal Bunny. It’s also Denis Farr. I’m an editor for GayGamer, which is at a curious intersection between LGBT concerns and gaming news.

I recruit and work with writers. Some want their names attached to their work, some don’t, and since we’re launching a redesign soon, we’re allowing both. We write about games. Sometimes that concerns sexuality, sometimes it doesn’t.

LGBT communities have required discretion, anonymity, and pseudonyms for some time. In an ideal world, there would be no concern for closets or talking about finding Chris Redfield or Poison hot or whom we romanced in Dragon Age. This is not an ideal world, and we want to be able to discuss these topics without worry. We also want to be able to interact amongst ourselves using the names we have claimed from one network and community to another.

While it would be nice to live in a world where I do not have to wonder whether including my work with GayGamer on a résumé will get me work, I don’t live in that world. Further, while I choose to write in a number of places, and use my name, I recognize that my writers do this for fun. They do this because they love games. They have jobs that are not related to gaming. Not all of them want their names attached to that work so they can be Googled at a moment’s whim.

Which speaks to our audience as well. Gaymers post comments. They interact with us on Twitter. They talk in our forums. Pseudonyms mean they don’t have to worry about people Googling their name and outing either their hobby or part of their identity. Nor do they need to worry about accidentally being outed when they haven’t made that decision yet.

Because multiplayer gaming is so widespread in gaming, and we enjoy playing games together, it can be easy to use the same handle and gamer tags from service to service. When we join networks to discuss games, it only makes sense to transfer that reputation. We are carving identities, and not just in the games we play, but in the ways we interact outside of games. Names are what we build in to them, and deeming one name more important than another seems a rather strange concept. It depends on what the purpose of a network is. We happen to use them to create a safe space online.
