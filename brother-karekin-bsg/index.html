My name is Brother Karekin, BSG

My name is BROTHER Karekin, BSG. Brother is not what I do. It is who I am. It is the way in which I have related to the world for nearly 20 years of my life. For me, and for many like me, brother is not a title. It is not an honorific. It is the very means by which we choose relate to others.

Introducing myself as Brother is a statement of relationship that is as integral to my identity in this world as the name I carry. When I first entered religious life, I legally changed my first name to represent the change in life, attitudes, and purpose to my life. Being a brother is so integral, in fact, that even my closest friends socially call me brother. The term “brother” is about my being, not about my activity. I have taken up this manner of life as a means of bring peace to the world, to my relationships, and in some small way to bring healing to the broken.

I have been the subject of film, I am an author, and a prominent religious figure in the LGBT community in San Francisco. Within this community, I have served the battered, the broken, and especially the resurrected; individuals who have taken new names to escape persecution, or to celebrate new found identities. I work a good deal with transgendered individuals for whom the prerogative to name themselves is akin to taking up a new skin. I have known drag queens who have raised countless thousands of dollars for charity under their pseudonyms, while their “real” names can, and do, go a lifetime without notice – often by choice.

California is one of those states that gives us the freedom to be known in any way we wish to be known. Our name in the world – our identity – is taken up by choice and not by design given the persecution so many people suffer. I stand with Anne Morrow Lindbergh who once said, “to name one’s self is the prerogative of poets and revolutionaries.” I am blessed to live as Brother among these my friends. I am humbled to live in religious witness among my LGBT brothers and sisters; with drag queens, and trans-men, and activists, and poets and revolutionaries who have taken up their names and decided their place in the world for themselves.

A “real names” policy is diminishing to those who have struggled to redefine themselves. It ignores the reality that we are who we say we are, and not necessarily what appears on our birth certificates or our drivers licenses. It ignores the reality of those who struggle to become who they are intended to be, in a world that oppresses and even kills them for doing so. It ignores how much some have to lose by being exposed to political oppression and social stigma were their “real” identities known. We all deserve the right to have decide for ourselves how we will be known in the world. Our names are part of the dignity of self-determination and should not be regulated as though we were merely commodities.

My name is Brother Karekin. My name is me.
