My name is Debbie Notkin

My name is Debbie Notkin. I’ve always been a little envious of what novelist Robertson Davies calls “the twice-born,” people who remake themselves and “often go so far as to take new names.” I’m not one of them. My name is a nickname based on “Deborah Notkin,” the name on all my legal ID. It’s what my friends call me, it’s what my bankers and employers call me, and it’s how most people know me. I have a couple of other online handles, all freely associated with my name.

One thing I do is help run WisCon, a 35-year-old feminist science fiction convention, held each year in Madison, Wisconsin, on Memorial Day weekend. This year, I’m actually not helping run it, because the rest of the committee was gracious enough to invite me to be a guest of honor. But I’ve been involved in running it for about fifteen years. People also know me through the books Women En Large: Images of Fat Nudes and Familiar Men: A Book of Nudes for which Laurie Toby Edison took the photographs and I wrote (or co-wrote) the text. The photo I’m using with this blog is a nude Laurie took of me many years ago; it’s the cover photo for Women En Large. Laurie and I also blog together on body image (widely interpreted) at Body Impolitic.

I was invited to contribute here because of WisCon, but the photography book experience is also relevant. Let’s start with WisCon. (I’m not speaking for WisCon, just about it.) At WisCon, any member can participate in the program (panels, readings, solo presentations) by signing up, and you can sign up under any name that suits you. You can also have any name that suits you on your badge. This is partly because we’re a feminist convention, and we see it as a matter of respect: you know what you want to be called, how you want to be referred to in convention reports, how you want to be remembered.

It’s also partly because of a much older science fiction convention tradition (science fiction conventions are about 75 years old now, so we get to have long traditions) called the “badge name.” Badge names can be for fun: an SF convention can be time out of time, a place to be a little different than your everyday persona, with a different name. And science fiction has had a long overlap with the Society for Creative Anachronism, a group in which everyone takes a different name. When I first got into science fiction, and met people from the SCA, forty(!) years ago, I often would learn someone’s SCA name before I knew their “mundane” name. Some people use badge names because they write under pseudonyms: if you don’t want your colleagues to know that you write “that rocket-ship stuff,” you write it under a name your employer won’t recognize, and that’s the name you want on your convention badge. For as long as I can recall, “badge name” has been a field on the form you fill out to join an SF convention.

The photography work situation is a little bit different. Laurie’s and my two best-known projects are books of nudes. Not everyone who is willing to be photographed nude wants to (or is safe choosing to) attach their drivers’ license name to a publicly displayed nude photograph. (These are not erotic nudes; they’re environmental portraits of people in their own spaces, living their lives. Nonetheless…) The first photograph in Women En Large is of a woman who was at the time a U.S. government employee. She got formal government permission to have her nude photograph published – as long as she didn’t use her whole name. Other people chose to use different names because they were teachers, or thought they might want to be president someday, or just didn’t want people they didn’t know to be able to associate their daily name with what they look like with their clothes off. I was pretty much self-employed at the time, my name was on the book anyway as a writer, and I knew I would never be president. Laurie and I both know that the books would be poorer if we had only used photographs of people whose names we could also use.

In my lifetime, I’ve had to learn that a person’s gender is the one (or more than one) they choose to identify with, that a person’s race and ethnicity are not apparent to the eye and must be evaluated by what they say about themselves, that whether or not a person is able-bodied is something that person knows better than anyone else. But I don’t remember having to learn that a person’s name is what they call themselves: that’s been part of my life for so long that I can’t believe we’re still arguing about it.
