My name is Violet Blue

My name is Violet Blue. I’m the author and editor of over 30 published books in several languages, a tech columnist for CBS Interactive/ZDNet, a Forbes Web Celeb, an awarded blogger and podcaster, media pundit on privacy, sexuality and technology, and much more. I presented a Google Tech Talk at Google Inc. about sexual privacy online; the video has received over 1.5 million views. I’m also a member of the Internet Press Guild, a teacher and volunteer sexuality crisis counselor at nonprofit SFSI.org, and on the Board of Advisors at nonprofit organization Without My Consent.

In my work as a book and magazine editor in print and online, I have had the privilege to work with literally hundreds of authors and writers. Because the topic is most often women’s issues, human sexuality, gender, and LGBT themes, most of the authors I have worked with use pseudonyms. They do so for a variety of reasons.

It is my responsibility to protect the real names and identities of these writers – as has been the role and tradition of editors for hundreds of years. I take this responsibility very seriously.

It is my belief that if writers could not express their work freely within the safety of pseudonyms, we would not have some of the greatest literary works, most groundbreaking writing, or necessary cultural evolutionary leaps through investigative journalism we benefit from today as a society – if it were not for the ability of a writer to use a pseudonym.

I have edited and contributed to a number of high-profile blogs in the past ten years, and I see the same landscape.

In my experience, it is a misnomer to believe that people only use pseudonyms or handles if they are behaving with malfeasance. I have witnessed the opposite. I believe that it is the abuse of the few – stalkers, harassers and trolls – that has poisoned the image of the majority of pseudonym users: everyday people that simply want to create a necessary separation in their lives.

In my work at SFSI.org, I have staffed the call center – a hotline for people to call when they are in a personal health crisis, which often crosses over into relationship crises and gender / sexual identity crises. Here, I see the spectrum of adults and teens whose identities must not be compromised for fear of persecution, or worse.

At Without My Consent, we offer legal and life resources for people – primarily women – that have had material published online with the intent to significantly harm them. This organization has grown out of an ever-increasing flood of legal cases lawyers are finding themselves faced with where the victim they represent is a Jane Doe, and the perpetrator is a person whose real name is known.

It is striking how little a deterrent knowing the identity of the perpetrator actually is; their sole intent is to terrorize, humiliate and harass the victim.

Even with knowing the identity of the perpetrator, there is never a clear path to justice for the victim. The legal system is currently poorly set up to help victims of real-life abuse, harassment and attacks; it is even worse for the online victim – whose personal information (such as their name) can be found and exploited for abuse all too easily.

Each person has their own strategies around controlling their identity and safeguarding their personal lives, and protecting the lives of those close to them. The ability to use pseudonyms is essential in many, if not most people’s, strategies.

When I was a columnist for the San Francisco Chronicle, I received a few serious death threats – as did the female sports columnist, who got more abuse and harassment than I ever did. The people that threatened me, and two that have gone on to harass and stalk me to this day, have done so with their real names proudly on display. It has made updating their police files simple, but does not make my life a safe place to be. They used sock puppets but abandoned them when they received attention, and gladly take public credit for their attacks on me and my loved ones with their real names.

Pseudonyms are vital for the protection of all people that are targets: women, at-risk youth, minorities, abuse and violence survivors, political activists, lesbians, gay men, transgender people, bisexual people, public figures (especially female public figures), writers, people that simply want to keep their private lives private.

My name really is Violet Blue. Despite any rubbish you’ve seen by my harassers and detractors, Violet Blue is the name on my passport, social security card, all my ID, and it is who I am. I have never pretended to be anyone else (though some people have tried to pretend to be me) nor have I ever used a pseudonym.

But I recognize through my work and personal life that we are in an online society where decisions are made by people that do not understand that pseudonyms are a matter of life and death for some, and a matter of necessity for others.

And to that, I have to stand up and fight.

Violet Blue
Author, columnist, advisor
