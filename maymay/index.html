My name is Maymay

My name is Maymay. I’m a social justice technologist and sexual freedom activist, founder of the KinkForAll.org series of sexuality education unconferences, co-author of several web development books, and frequent public speaker on issues ranging from censorship to gender expression.

Although my legal name is different, “maymay” has been just as much my real name since I was 9 years old. Given to me by an online penpal, it quickly became more than just a nickname; it’s also the tool I use most often to protect my privacy. First as a young person on the Internet, then as a sexuality subculture community member, and now as a political activist, I deliberately used my chosen name in place of my given name as a way to ensure I had control over my identity—and my privacy.

Most people incorrectly think of privacy as though it is secrecy—the inability to know a particular piece of information—but in fact privacy is context management. For a time, it was equally unsafe for me to be “maymay” at my day job as it was for me to be “Meitar” (my legal name) in other contexts. Young people are routinely admonished for giving out personal information online, but the services they use increasingly demand that very data. People whose gender or sexuality differs from convention face the abrupt loss of their livelihood, homes, and sometimes their loved ones when their identity-in-context is breached by another.

Having the power to choose a pseudonym isn’t just an Internet tradition, it’s a user-centered design pattern that actively nurtures diversity, creativity, and self-empowerment. Whenever you hear someone say, “Oh, is that what the kids are calling it these days?” you’re listening to a discussion about censorship and a very simple circumvention tool called slang. The same is true whenever you hear someone use a pseudonym.
