My name is sniffles

My name is sniffles. I am a user experience strategist/designer and a long-time grassroots advocate for best web practices and standards. I’m the editor-in-chief for Web Standards Sherpa, working with top web specialists to provide practical techniques towards building a more engaging and equitable web. I am also a trainer and speaker on user experience, web quality, product development and team management.

My name comes from an era where usernames on Unix platforms had an eight character limit. Old friends from the university network still call me by this name today, as do acquaintances from Usenet and IRC who became friends. Now, Twitter friends would call me “sniffles” when they greet me in real life.

As a woman on early IRC days, a non-gender specific username protected me from harassment and misogynic situations. Most of all, it allowed me to have fair and intelligent conversation with others without gender bias. Many would be surprised to later discover I am femaleâ€”on a couple of occasions they were convinced I was a bot rather than a real woman online.

In reality, I have several real names. The name on my birth certificate bears no resemblance to the name on my passport; I was given a different name later in childhood, and then I changed my name from a previous marriage as an adult.

Born with a culturally different name and spending my formative years in a Western country, I was aware of name discrimination very early. It goes beyond childhood jeers and mispronunciations by teachers. Our true names can betray us and alienate us, amplify prejudice and racism. They can diminish opportunities for employment and deny us fair consideration despite our qualifications. These problems are very real; part of creating an equitable web means allowing people to use their pseudonyms, acknowledging that these names are as important and as genuine as names on paper. The name on paper was one given to us; pseudonyms are what we grew into, what we chose and who we have become.
