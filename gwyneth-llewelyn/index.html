My name is Gwyneth Llewelyn

My name is Gwyneth Llewelyn, and I’m a virtual girl in a virtual world. I’m too old to still be called a “geek”. But back in the early 1990s I was one of the top Internet evangelists in my own country; these days, I have the same enthusiasm in promoting online virtual worlds and a lifestyle based on providing services over them, which naturally also raises the issue of identity.

In early 2004 I was stalked over the Internet. A small group of petty criminals, who were actually not very computer-literate, nevertheless managed to figure out, based on IP address and other similar, simple, easy-to-find hints, if I was at home, at work, or someplace else. This allowed them to track me down in order to blackmail me; I had earned the reputation of being rich (a rumour spread by a former associate) and this encouraged all sorts of small criminals, scammers, and others to help me to relieve me from the alleged vast fortune I had amassed during the golden days of the Internet revolution…

Because police work in my country is too slow — and the criminals know that — due to judiciary bureaucracy, I had no other option but to move far, far away from my home, leaving my contacts to my lawyer and close family, and live in a tiny rented room on a flat with little else but a bed, a tiny fridge, a micro-wave oven, and a shower in the bathroom. I had no fixed job, a salary below the poverty line, and not a single cent in my name; all I had was some clothes and a computer.

Since most of my work was done remotely, I made a decision: I would shut down all messengers, stop participating on forums, chatrooms, and similar online venues under my own name. I even stopped blogging, something I had done irregularly since 1995. Instead, I went completely pseudonymous, thanks mostly to Second Life, which I had “discovered” in mid-2004. In Second Life, nobody really knows who you are — and nobody truly cares. So slowly I started picking up all my social networking functions, including blogging, and even started participating online far more than before. But this time it was under my pseudonym.

Ironically, I even created a new company, where nobody but the partners knew my true identity. Contracts were signed with my pseudonym; meetings with customers were mostly done online (in Second Life) or just by exchanging emails or text messages. The company is still around and doing business, and very few know my real name or identity, but that was never an obstacle in providing good services and establishing a solid reputation.

I gave some live interviews (and lots of written ones) and did remote presentations using my avatar; I wrote an article for the Guardian under my pseudonym (as well for many sites as a guest blogger); I even have a few academic articles waiting to be published (and a few references from fellow researchers) and to this very day, almost all online registrations I constantly do use my pseudonym pretty much everywhere. Beyond Second Life, I’m also active in the WordPress community. The result of all this activity is the oddity that Google or Bing list ten or twenty times more results for my pseudonym than for my so-called “real” identity, which is pretty much “invisible” on the Internet.

Living for seven years under an assumed pseudonym which has a much vaster impact on the ‘net audience than my real identity started to make me question issues about identity and, ultimately, the self. Perhaps it’s no coincidence that I’ve also started to become a serious Dharma practitioner, which definitely helps one to experimentally find answers about one’s self, and these are radically opposite to what the likes of Facebook and Google think about identity
