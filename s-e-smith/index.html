My name is s.e. smith

My name is s.e. smith. I am a writer, commentator, and activist with publication credits ranging from Bitch Magazine to The Guardian; some people know me from blogs like FWD/Forward: Feminists with disabilities for a way forward and Tiger Beatdown, as well as my home base, this ain’t livin’.

I started blogging regularly in 2001, starting this ain’t livin’ in 2005, where I originally wrote under a pseudonym, meloukhia. In 2009, I merged my personal, political, and professional identities and started using my name on all my writing.

For me, it was a dangerous decision. Being an activist can be dangerous, and it can be especially dangerous when your identity becomes a weapon that can be used against you. I am a person with multiple disabilities, including a highly stigmatised mental health condition. My decision to write openly under my own name means that I cannot, ever, disclose my mental health condition, unless I want to expose myself to considerable risk.

Many people with mental illnesses who choose to write about them are forced to do so pseudonymously because writing openly could endanger them professionally or personally. A mental health condition is still grounds, in many regions, to declare someone an unfit parent and take children away. A parent blogging about parenting and membership in the mad pride movement risks an intervention from child protective services. An academic who discloses a mental health condition may never earn tenure. Professionals like attorneys and doctors cannot jeapordise their careers by disclosing mental health diagnoses.

I am fortunate in that much of my work focuses on social justice topics, and that I work with progressive editors who are not discomfited by working with writers with mental illness, let alone running our bylines in major publications. The same privilege does not extend to all people with mental illness who want to develop careers and establish themselves, which is why the ability to identify pseudonymously on the Internet is so critical; you do not need to use your legal name to matter, to have a voice, to have a say, to fight stigma, to engage in activism, to communicate, to self-advocate, to exchange ideas, or even to freely associate.

People can engage in acts of great evil anonymously and pseudonymously. They also do so when identified by their legal names. The solution to evil is to fight evil, not the names people use when they commit it. Forcing people to use their legal names would drive many marginalised people underground, many of my fellow disabled and transgender people among them. It would silence many voices that need to be heard, and it would deprive people of one of the most sacred rights of all: The right to self identify and define your own lived experience.
