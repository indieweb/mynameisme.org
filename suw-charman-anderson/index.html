My name is Suw

My name is Suw. I’m the founder of Ada Lovelace Day, an international day celebrating the achievements of women in science, technology, engineering and maths. I’m also the co-founder of the Open Rights Group and I work as a journalist and social technologist.

My name is important to me, although my relationship with it has been a complex one. As a teen, I intensely disliked my first and middle names – Susan Margaret – because they seemed to belong to another, older, generation. I spent a lot of energy trying to come up with another name that I felt more accurately represented who I was. It wasn’t until some ten years later that, whilst working in publishing, I made the typo that took my Sue and turned it into a unique three letter identifier that reflected more clearly my sense of self.

Names aren’t trivial things, no matter how we come by them. They can strongly shape the way we view ourselves and the way others view us. But there isn’t a bright dividing line between “real” names and pseudonyms. Suw is not a standard shortening of Susan, so does that make Suw a pseudonym? Certainly I cannot fly on airline tickets booked in the name of Suw.

My first online pseudonym was a male name, picked not quite at random. I didn’t realise when I chose it that having a bloke’s name would give me a degree of protection. And although I never made any great efforts to conceal my gender, many people were surprised when they discovered I was a woman.

I chose, eventually, to merge my two identities, but I have no doubt that my male name smoothed my early online experiences, especially in chat rooms. I would go as far as to say that that I wouldn’t be the Suw I am if I hadn’t had a chance to be Lewis. (And for some people, a decade and a half later, I still am Lewis.)

Pseudonyms are important. They act as an identity sandbox that allows us to figure out who we are. They protect us from casual misogyny and bigotry. In some cases, they protect life and limb from malicious actors. And, occasionally, the pseudonym loses its pseudo and becomes, simply, our name. Had I not stumbled across my W and found that it expressed my identity so perfectly, I would very likely have opened this piece with the statement “My name is Lewis.”

In the end, I made peace with my full name. I discovered one day that Susan is from the Hebrew for ‘lily’, and Margaret is from the Greek for ‘pearl’. As a blonde with a pale complexion, to find that my parents had named me after two white things finally gave me a deep, nerdy joy. But the name I go by in day-to-day life remains my creation, my choice.

I believe that everyone has the right to self-determination. That means everyone has the right to choose their own name.
