My Name Is Flavia Dzodan

My name is Flavia Dzodan.  I live in Amsterdam, The Netherlands. I am a writer, an activist and ideas instigator who bases her political affiliations, activism, and work on the rights of minorities and immigrants living in Europe. My work has appeared in publications like The Guardian and I am also an editor at Tiger Beatdown. I have been interviewed by major Dutch publications like De Volkskrant over my views and opinions of Dutch society. Additionally, I am a member of one of Amsterdam’s main political parties, where I am active within my community to raise awareness of discrimination and institutional practices that seek to alienate those of non-Dutch origin.

I write under my government name because I wish to give visibility to the realities of being a minority and an immigrant in an environment that is growing increasingly hostile to diversity and giving preeminence to outdated notions of nationalism and xenophobia. I have the privilege of writing under my government name because I am a documented, legal resident of the country I live in. However, this privilege is not awarded to everyone.

Political refugees fear for the fate of family, friends, and communities still living in their home countries. Bearing witness to the acts that forced them to leave their homes comes with a price. In cases like these, pseudonymity is not just a matter of hiding one’s identity but of protecting the very livelihood of those left behind.

Many come to Europe seeking asylum. North African migrants arrive at the coast of European countries and are placed in temporary internment camps, only to be released at a later date without a clear legal status. While their applications are processed, the asylum seeker is left voiceless, unable to speak of their experience. This whole process can take a decade or longer. Speaking about this injustice under one’s government name comes with great risk, the asylum seeker faces further internment in a camp or deportation. In these cases, a pseudonym, a new identity, is an absolute necessity.

Immigrants, both documented and undocumented, fear the consequences of speaking up against injustice and discrimination. Many are victims of crimes that they cannot report because doing so would bring their residency claims into question. Women migrants are systematically targeted for sexual harassment and abuse because perpetrators know that the chances of police involvement are minimal. If an immigrant wishes to seek support in online communities and safe spaces, doing so under their government name would put them under the spotlight of authorities.

By enforcing a policy of “Only government names are allowed”, we are denying all of these people a voice. We are confining them to a silenced category where we remove their right to even discuss the injustices inflicted upon them. We are complicit in their double victimization.
