My name is Albatross

My name is Albatross, and mine is possibly one of the older nom-de-plumes online.

I have used the online handle Albatross since I began using Minnesota’s educational computer network MECC in 1975, during the Ford Administration. MECC offered online chat, games, and discussion forums to Minnesota students decades before the Internet made such things commonplace.

In 1983 I created the company GamBit that produced the first commercial MMORPG, back when “MM” was 16 users, and “O” meant dial-up. “Scepter of Goth” is the great-grandfather of games such as “World of Warcraft” and “Everquest,” and my handle on my GamBit computer system was always Albatross.

In 1991 I co-authored the Internet Gopher protocol (RFC 1436) which was the predecessor to the World Wide Web. Search Google Groups for alt.gopher and albatross, and you’ll find my participation in Gopher development from that era.

I own the domain name Albatross.org, and my wedding ring (forged in 1988) features two albatrosses flying about my wife’s birthstone. Among other things I hold the Twitter handle “Albatross” and the Livejournal account “Albatross.”

As an adoptee, the name Albatross is important to me as the name I gave myself. My adoptive parents gave me my father’s name with “Jr.” appended. I didn’t learn the name that my birthmother gave me until I found her 32 years later. But Albatross is the name I gave myself. If you’re not adopted it may not be immediately apparent how important such identity can be.

I am not transgender, but from my friends in the transgender community I have learned the importance of addressing people in the manner that they ask to be addressed. If a person wants to be referred to by a particular name or gender pronoun, my transgender friends have shown me that it is simply courteous to do so. Likewise, it is simple courtesy to allow people to adopt whatever handles they wish in order to be addressed in online communications.

Additionally, there are no mechanisms for confirming that a “real-looking name” is authentic. If I say that my name is “Roger Sheffield” how is that any more authentic than “Albatross”? Unless online services intend to implement some kind of highly invasive identity verification, attempting to control names is a futile exercise to impose norms as well as a violation of privacy.

And the more rigorously “real” names are verified, the more dangerous will be those “real-looking” names that manage to game the system to gain false authenticity. Any organization that attempts to authenticate names takes on the liability when that authentication is defrauded.

Attempting to enforce “normal” names may be well-intentioned, but it is a fruitless and poorly-thought-out endeavor that protects no one and exposes the authenticating agency to liability. Internet use has grown for twenty years, and most Internet users will hopefully have developed a perfectly healthy skepticism of identity on the Internet – either that, or they believe they have several e-mail friends who are deposed African princes and presidents. Rather than waste time enforcing the unenforceable and authenticating the inauthenticatable, that energy would be better directed towards teaching people how to better protect themselves online.

Bob Alberti
AKA Albatross
